Joint Planning and the Battle of Okinawa (1945) Case Study

The 82-day Okinawa campaign (April-June 1945) was the largest amphibious assault of the Pacific War and the last major battle of World War II. It remains a premier case study in joint operational planning.

CENTER OF GRAVITY: Japanese defense relied on deeply fortified positions in southern Okinawa (Shuri Line). The joint force identified and systematically reduced these through combined naval gunfire, aerial bombardment, and ground assault.

JOINT COMMAND STRUCTURE: Admiral Nimitz (naval) held overall command with General Buckner (ground) leading Tenth Army. The joint command integrated US Navy, Marine Corps, Army, and Allied naval forces under unified operational control.

SUSTAINMENT: The operation sustained 183,000 troops across 1,200 miles of ocean for 82 continuous days of combat. The logistics pipeline required coordination between Army service forces and Navy supply chains, demonstrating the criticality of joint sustainment planning.

TEMPO AND CASUALTIES: Highest single-battle casualties of the Pacific campaign. US losses: over 12,000 killed, 38,000 wounded. Japanese losses: approximately 110,000 troops, plus an estimated 100,000+ civilian casualties. The kamikaze campaign inflicted heavy naval losses.

LESSONS:
1. Joint command relationships must be clearly defined before operations commence
2. Sustainment planning is as critical as maneuver planning in joint operations
3. Physical terrain can be shaped (island fortification) to offset maneuver disadvantage
4. Civilian population considerations must be integrated into operational planning
5. Joint fires coordination between naval gunfire, air, and ground requires dedicated liaison
